# Report Logic

## 1. Establish Boundary Before Analysis

For each chapter or section, first make clear:

- analysis object;
- spatial boundary and affected receptors;
- time boundary such as construction period, operation period, flood season, dry season, accident scenario, or planning horizon;
- indicators and evaluation criteria;
- evidence sources;
- what the section will not determine if it is outside scope.

Weak pattern:

> This section analyzes the environmental impact of the project.

Preferred pattern:

> This section focuses on the possible water environment impact during the operation period. Based on current monitoring, design discharge conditions and model prediction results, it analyzes concentration increment, affected reach, sensitive sections and management requirements under normal and unfavorable hydrological conditions.

## 2. Keep Evidence, Analysis, Judgement, and Measures Separate

Use this order unless the user provides a different report convention:

1. Evidence: monitoring results, survey records, model settings, design documents, applicable standards, or expert comments.
2. Analysis: comparison among sections, years, scenarios, indicators, or alternatives.
3. Judgement: what the evidence supports and under what conditions.
4. Measures: what should be monitored, controlled, optimized, or verified.

Avoid mixing a fact and a management conclusion in one long sentence.

## 3. Support Conclusions With Traceable Basis

Environmental consulting conclusions should point to at least one basis:

- monitoring data or historical records;
- field survey;
- model simulation;
- experimental or pilot results;
- policy, standard, plan, or approval document;
- engineering design document;
- expert opinion;
- comparable case;
- reasonable technical inference with assumptions stated.

Do not write "the impact is generally controllable" without explaining impact object, range, condition, and control measures.

## 4. Compare With One Evaluation Caliber

When comparing multiple areas, years, alternatives, models, sections, scenarios, or indicators:

- use the same statistical period;
- use the same unit and data source;
- explain whether comparing mean value, maximum value, increment, load, compliance rate, exceedance multiple, or risk level;
- avoid selecting different metrics only to highlight different advantages;
- explain primary and secondary indicators if results point in different directions.

## 5. End Sections With Closure

A section summary should answer:

- what has been clarified;
- what remains uncertain;
- how the result supports later prediction, design optimization, risk control, monitoring, or approval communication.

Weak pattern:

> In summary, the above analysis is relatively comprehensive.

Preferred pattern:

> In summary, this section clarifies the main sensitive receptors, current concentration level and unfavorable scenario for the project area. These results provide the basis for subsequent scenario setting, impact prediction and follow-up monitoring requirements.
